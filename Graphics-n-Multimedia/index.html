01
INTRODUCTION TO ANIMATION
01
"Rapidly displaying sequential still images to create the ILLUSION OF MOVEMENT — exploiting the brain's own perception quirks."
01
🔤 DEFINITION
From Latin anima — "to give soul".

• Series of still images shown rapidly
• 24 fps = cinema standard
• 30 fps = TV broadcast
• 60 fps = modern gaming
• Below 12 fps → visible flicker

FIRST ANIMATION: Eadweard Muybridge's horse gallop (1878)
02
👁 PERSISTENCE OF VISION
Physical effect in the human eye.

• Retina retains image ~50ms after it disappears
• Afterimage overlaps the next frame
• Creates seamless continuous flow

DISCOVERED: Peter Mark Roget (1824)

Brain cannot distinguish individual frames at 24+ fps
03
🧠 PHI PHENOMENON
Purely psychological motion effect.

• Brain infers motion between two static objects in rapid sequence
• Does NOT need retinal retention

DISCOVERED: Max Wertheimer (1912)

Classic examples:
• Flip-book animation
• Traffic "flowing" arrows
• Neon sign sequences
04
✨ ILLUSION OF MOVEMENT
Combined result of BOTH effects.

POV alone ≠ enough  |  Phi alone ≠ enough
Together → seamless motion

The brain WANTS to see motion — animators exploit this biological "glitch" in human perception.

This is why animation feels real even though it's completely artificial.

03
ANIMATION TECHNIQUES
03
🖊 CEL ANIMATION
Traditional hand-drawn technique.

• Drawings on transparent celluloid sheets
• Photographed frame-by-frame
• Background reuse saves cost

ERA: 1910s – 1990s
STUDIOS: Disney, Warner Bros, Toei
📍 PATH ANIMATION
Object follows a predefined trajectory.

• Animator defines path shape
• Easing controls speed variation
• Object can deform along path

TOOLS: After Effects, CSS, SVG paths
💻 COMPUTER ANIMATION
Digital creation of moving images.

• Simulates cel & path techniques
• Plus: physics, particles, simulation
• Real-time OR pre-rendered output

ERA: 1980s – present
STUDIOS: Pixar, DreamWorks, ILM
04
TYPES OF ANIMATION
04
Named after CELLULOID (1869). Developed ~1914 by Raoul Barré & John Bray. Path animation: object follows a predefined Bézier trajectory with full easing control.
01
🎞 CEL ANIMATION
• Transparent sheet per frame; BG separate
• Colors applied to BACK of cel
• ~12–24 unique cels per second

USED IN: Tom & Jerry, Snow White, Dragon Ball Z
02
🔑 KEYFRAMES
The "anchor" frames of an action.

• Show most extreme / critical poses
• Drawn by SENIOR animators
• Define timing & structure

EXAMPLE:
Frame 1 → standing
Frame 12 → peak of jump ↑
Frame 24 → landed ↓

Modern software uses same keyframe concept digitally
03
🔄 TWEENING (In-Betweening)
Frames drawn BETWEEN keyframes.

• Creates smooth motion transition
• Done by JUNIOR animators
• More tweens = smoother motion

PENCIL TEST: Quick rough sketch preview — validates timing before expensive inking

AUTO-TWEEN: Adobe Animate, Toon Boom, After Effects
04
📍 PATH ANIMATION
Object follows a predefined trajectory. Bézier curves = smooth & organic movement.
EASING: slow in → fast → slow out
TOOLS: After Effects, CSS, SVG, Unity
05
COMPUTER ANIMATION
05
Computer animation uses software to create motion — simulating traditional techniques or producing effects impossible by hand. Revolutionized the industry from 1980s onward.
✏ CEL   vs   💻 COMPUTER
✏ CEL
Hand-drawn on physical sheets
Every frame manually drawn
Physical film storage needed
Expensive: cels, paint, camera
Difficult to modify frames
Organic hand-crafted warmth
VS
💻 COMPUTER
✓ Created in digital software
✓ Automation handles tweens
✓ Instant backup & sharing
✓ One-time software cost
✓ Modify any frame instantly
✓ Any style — cartoon to CG
🦾 FORWARD KINEMATICS (FK)
🤖 INVERSE KINEMATICS (IK)
🔮 MORPHING

12 PRINCIPLES  •  Part 1 / 2
07
By Disney masters Ollie Johnston & Frank Thomas (The Illusion of Life, 1981). The universal gold standard — used by every professional animator on Earth.
01
01 — SQUASH & STRETCH
Exaggerate deformation for weight & life.
RULE: Volume stays constant. The most fundamental principle.
02
02 — ANTICIPATION
Crouch before jump. Swing back before throw.
Without it → actions feel sudden & fake
03
03 — STAGING
Present ONE clear idea at a time.

• Clear, readable silhouettes
• Camera angle serves the story
• Avoid distracting backgrounds
• Direct the audience's eye

Think: theater staging, not photography.
04
04 — STRAIGHT AHEAD & POSE-TO-POSE
STRAIGHT AHEAD: Draw frame by frame start to end — spontaneous, fluid, surprising

POSE-TO-POSE: Key poses first, fill in betweens — controlled, planned, consistent

Best work combines BOTH strategically.
05
05 — FOLLOW THROUGH & OVERLAPPING
FOLLOW THROUGH: Parts keep moving after main action stops. Hair swings after head stops.

OVERLAPPING: Different parts move at different rates. Arms lag behind torso.

Makes motion feel organic & believable.
06
06 — EASE IN & EASE OUT
Objects accelerate at start, decelerate at end.
Linear = robotic  |  Ease = natural weight
07
12 PRINCIPLES  •  Part 2 / 2
07
Walt Disney: "The secret of animation is the exaggeration of the truth." These final 6 principles deliver exactly that truth.
07
07 — ARCS
Natural motion follows ARC paths, not straight lines. Arms swing in arcs. Breaking arcs → instantly looks mechanical & stiff.
08
08 — SECONDARY ACTION
Supporting actions that ENRICH the primary movement.

Walking + arms swing (secondary)
Running + hair flows (secondary)
Talking + eyebrow moves (secondary)

RULE: Never overpower the main action.
09
09 — TIMING
Number of frames = speed, weight & personality.
AT 24fps: Blink = 6–8f  • Head turn = 10–15f
Timing is the HEARTBEAT of animation.
10
10 — EXAGGERATION
Push actions BEYOND realistic limits for emotional impact.

• Giant cartoon eyes & reactions
• Impossible stretching
• Over-the-top expressions

KEY: Making TRUTH more VISIBLE & ENTERTAINING. A sad character cries buckets, not a few tears.
11
11 — SOLID DRAWING
Characters must feel like 3D objects with real weight & form.

• Understand spheres, cubes, cylinders
• "Draw through" the form
• Avoid "twinning" (mirror poses)
• Strong line of action

Even in 2D — audience must FEEL volume
12
12 — APPEAL
Characters must be visually interesting to WATCH.

APPEAL ≠ CUTENESS: A villain, monster, anti-hero — all can have appeal.

Clear design • Dynamic poses • Charisma • Strong silhouette

TEST: Would the audience keep watching?
08
ANIMATION SPACE
08
Animation can exist in three spatial dimensions — each with different tools, techniques, visual results, and hardware requirements.
2D SPACE (X + Y)
Width + Height only. No depth.

• Classic cartoon aesthetic
• Simple to create & render
• Low hardware requirements

EXAMPLES: Tom & Jerry, Mario, Pac-Man
TOOLS: Adobe Animate, Toon Boom, Spine
2½D SPACE (pseudo-3D)
2D art + ILLUSION of depth via parallax layers.

• Z-axis visual without true 3D
• Great performance / visual tradeoff

EXAMPLES: Paper Mario, Ori and the Blind Forest
TOOLS: After Effects Z-depth, Unity 2D
3D SPACE (X + Y + Z)
Full volumetric environment. True depth.

• Any camera angle; real lighting
• Requires powerful hardware

EXAMPLES: Toy Story, Frozen, Fortnite
TOOLS: Blender, Maya, Cinema 4D, Unreal

ANIMATION PROCESS
09
Every animation project — from a 10-second ad to a 2-hour feature film — follows this same 4-stage pipeline.
📋
STEP 1
ORGANIZE EXECUTION
• Define project goals & scope
• Assign team roles
• Set milestones & deadlines
• Gather reference material
• Create style guide
• Budget & risk planning

OUTPUT: Project bible, schedule, storyboard
›
🛠
STEP 2
CHOOSE TOOL
• Match tool to project type
• Consider team skill level
• Account for budget

2D: Adobe Animate, Toon Boom
3D: Maya, Blender, Cinema 4D
Motion: After Effects
Games: Unity, Unreal
›
🎨
STEP 3
BUILD & TWEAK
• Create rough blocking first
• Refine with keyframes
• Apply the 12 Principles
• Sync with audio / music
• Team review cycles
• Iterate until approved

NOTE: This step is ITERATIVE
›
🎬
STEP 4
POST-PROCESS
• Render final frames
• Color grading & correction
• Add VFX & overlays
• Sound design & music mix
• Quality assurance testing
• Format for delivery
• Archive project files

OUTPUT: MP4, MOV, WebM
📌 SUMMARY — KEY TAKEAWAYS
01
🎬 Introduction
Animation = sequential images exploiting Persistence of Vision + Phi Phenomenon to create the illusion of seamless motion.
02
🎨 Usage
Art, storytelling, scientific visualization, and instructional training. Animation is used across every major industry worldwide.
03
🖊 Techniques
Three core techniques: Cel (hand-drawn), Path (trajectory-based), Computer (digital — most versatile today).
04
📽 Types
Keyframes + tweening = smooth motion. Path animation uses Bézier curves. Pencil test validates timing early.
05
💻 Computer Anim.
FK/IK chains for character rigs. Morphing for shape transforms. Far faster and more flexible than traditional cel.
06
⭐ Levels
Basic (GIFs/CSS) → Intermediate (motion graphics) → Advanced (feature films, real-time VFX, AI animation).
07
📐 12 Principles
Disney's Squash & Stretch, Anticipation, Timing, Ease, Arcs, and 7 more — the universal animation gold standard.
08
🌐 Animation Space
2D (flat) → 2½D (pseudo-depth via parallax) → 3D (full volumetric space, any camera angle, photorealism).
09
⚙ Process
Organize → Choose Tool → Build & Tweak (iterative) → Post-Process. Every project, every studio, worldwide.

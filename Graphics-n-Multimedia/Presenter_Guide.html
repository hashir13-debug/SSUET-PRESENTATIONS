SLIDE 3
01 · INTRODUCTION TO ANIMATION
↗ Open Slide 3
  • ▶Frame diagram at top: "See these 5 boxes — each one is a single photograph. Show them at 24 per second and your brain sees smooth motion. That's animation."
  • ▶Definition card: "Still images + rapid display = illusion of motion. Two brain effects make this work."
  • ▶Persistence of Vision (purple box): "Physical effect in your eye — when you see a bright image, your retina holds it for about 50 milliseconds AFTER it disappears. The next frame arrives before the first one fades."
  • ▶Phi Phenomenon (green box): "Psychological effect — your brain INFERS motion between two still objects. Even if your eye has no retention, your brain fills in the gap."
  • ▶FPS badges: "24 fps = cinema gold standard since the 1920s. 30 = TV. 60 = modern gaming. Below 12 fps and you start seeing flicker — your brain can no longer fake the motion."
📖 REAL EXAMPLES TO MENTION:
Persistence of Vision: Stare at a bright light, then look away — you still see it for a moment. That's POV.
Phi Phenomenon: A neon sign showing an arrow "flowing" left — it's just 3 static bulbs turning on and off. Your brain creates the motion.
FPS in real life: Old Indian films were shot at 24fps. Pakistani PTV dramas were 30fps. PUBG Mobile runs at 60fps. If your phone drops below 12fps during a game, it starts "lagging" — you see the individual frames.
First ever animation: Muybridge (1878) took 12 photos of a horse galloping. Played back fast — first animation in history.

SLIDE 6
04A · CEL ANIMATION TYPES
↗ Open Slide 6
  • ▶Stack diagram: "Background stays still — mountains, a room, a street. Then transparent CEL 1 goes on top with the character base. CEL 2 adds detail. CEL 3 adds expressions. All stacked = 1 frame."
  • ▶KEYFRAMES: "Senior animators draw ONLY the most important poses. Character standing still — that's keyframe 1. Peak of the jump — keyframe 12. Landed — keyframe 24."
  • ▶TWEENING: "Junior animators fill the 22 frames between those 3 keyframes. More tweening = smoother. Software like Adobe Animate does this automatically today."
  • ▶PENCIL TEST: "Before spending 3 weeks painting 500 cel sheets, they quickly film rough pencil sketches to check if the timing is right. Saves enormous cost."
🎨 CEL ANIMATION FACTS:
Tom & Jerry (1940s): Background artists repainted the SAME kitchen background hundreds of times — just changing the angle. Characters were on separate cels.
Cost: Snow White (1937) cost $1.4 million — equivalent to ~$30 million today. Most of the cost was painting 2 million cel frames by hand.
Keyframe example: A character throwing a ball: Frame 1 = arm back (keyframe). Frame 8 = arm at peak (keyframe). Frame 16 = arm forward, ball released (keyframe). Everything in between = tweening.
🎯 DEMO: Hold your hands flat on top of each other like stacked papers — "this is how cels were physically stacked and photographed"

SLIDE 8
05 · COMPUTER ANIMATION + FK/IK
↗ Open Slide 8
  • ▶Table, row by row: "Hand-drawn vs digital. Manual frame-by-frame vs auto-tweening. Physical film cans in a warehouse vs a digital file on Google Drive. Months per minute vs real-time preview."
  • ▶FK diagram (left): "Forward Kinematics — move the shoulder joint, elbow automatically follows, then wrist, then fingers. Like controlling a puppet — you move the top, everything below follows."
  • ▶IK diagram (right): "Inverse Kinematics — you just PLACE the hand where you want it, and the software automatically figures out where the elbow and shoulder need to be. Like reaching for a cup — you aim your hand, your brain calculates the rest."
  • ▶Morphing box: "Map 50 points on face A, same 50 points on face B — software interpolates. Michael Jackson's Black or White video (1991) — 8 different people morphing into each other. First time the world saw morphing."
🎮 FK vs IK — REAL WORLD:
FK Example: A robot arm in a factory — you move each joint precisely. A puppet show — control the top joints manually. Flag waving in wind — FK because the pole drives the cloth below.
IK Example: Your arm right now — reach for your phone without thinking about your shoulder angle. That's IK. Spider-Man climbing a wall — animator places his hands on the wall, IK calculates the body pose automatically.
Morphing: T-1000 in Terminator 2 (liquid metal). Logo reveals. Face filters on Snapchat = realtime morphing using IK + morphing together.
🎯 DEMO: Physically demonstrate IK — reach for your water bottle and say "I aimed for the bottle, my brain calculated all the joint angles — that's exactly how IK works in software."

SLIDE 10
07A · 12 PRINCIPLES — PART 1 (1–6)
↗ Open Slide 10
  • ▶Start: "These 12 principles were documented by Disney animators Ollie Johnston & Frank Thomas in their 1981 book 'The Illusion of Life'. Every professional animator on Earth still uses them."
  • ▶01 Squash & Stretch: "Deform for weight and life. Ball SQUASHES flat when it hits the ground — because it has mass, it can't be rigid. Ball STRETCHES tall when it's moving fast — because of velocity. Golden rule: VOLUME stays constant. A squashed ball gets wider as it gets flatter."
  • ▶02 Anticipation: "Small OPPOSITE action before main action. A baseball pitcher WINDS UP before throwing. A character CROUCHES before jumping. It mentally prepares the audience for what's coming."
  • ▶03 Staging: "One clear idea at a time. Use a clear silhouette the audience can read instantly. Camera angle serves the story."
  • ▶04 Straight Ahead vs Pose-to-Pose: "SA = draw every single frame from start to finish — spontaneous but hard to control. PtP = draw key poses first (like sketching the checkpoints), then fill in — controlled and planned."
  • ▶05 Follow Through & Overlap: "When a character stops running, their hair, coat, and scarf keep moving for a moment. Different parts stop at different times."
  • ▶06 Ease In/Out: "Nothing in nature starts at full speed or stops instantly. A car accelerates, then decelerates. This single principle makes the biggest difference between amateur and professional animation."
🏀 EXAMPLES FOR EACH PRINCIPLE:
Squash & Stretch: A bouncing ball. A cat landing from a jump. A face making an extreme expression — cheeks puffing out (stretch), eyes squinting (squash).
Anticipation: Bowler in cricket winds arm back first. Tom & Jerry — Tom raises fist back before slamming. Looney Tunes character runs off a cliff but pauses (anticipation) before falling.
Staging: In drama, a villain is shown from a low angle (looking up at them = powerful). Hero shown from above = vulnerable. Black silhouette against white background = instantly readable.
Ease In/Out: Drag a window in Windows — notice how it slows when you drop it? That's ease out. Apps opening on iPhone? Ease in then ease out. Remove it and everything feels "robotic."
🎯 DEMO: Physically squash your fist against your palm — show squash. Then do a "throwing" motion with your arm — show anticipation winding up. The audience will NEVER forget these two principles.
SLIDE 11
07B · 12 PRINCIPLES — PART 2 (7–12)
↗ Open Slide 11
  • ▶07 Arcs: "All natural motion follows curved paths. Swing your arm right now — it follows an arc. Eyes moving left to right follow a tiny arc. Throw a ball — parabola arc. ONLY robots and machines move in perfect straight lines — that's how you tell a badly animated robot from a good one."
  • ▶08 Secondary Action: "Walking is the main action. Arms swinging is secondary. Hair bouncing is secondary. Talking is main — eyebrows moving is secondary. The secondary enriches the main but must NEVER overpower it."
  • ▶09 Timing: "Number of frames = weight and personality. Eye blink = 6 frames (very fast). Head turn = 12 frames (medium). Heavy object falling = very few frames (fast). A feather falling = many frames (slow). Wrong timing and nothing else matters."
  • ▶10 Exaggeration: "Not about being unrealistic — about making the TRUTH more visible. A sad person cries a little. A SAD cartoon character cries a RIVER. Same emotion, exaggerated for impact."
  • ▶11 Solid Drawing: "Characters must feel like 3D objects even in 2D. Think in cubes and spheres. Avoid 'twinning' — where both arms do the exact same thing mirrored — it looks unnatural."
  • ▶12 Appeal: "A CHARACTER must be interesting to WATCH. Not just cute — a powerful villain has appeal. A complex anti-hero has appeal. Strong silhouette + personality + readable expression = appeal."
🎬 REAL FILM EXAMPLES:
Arcs: In every Pixar film, if you pause on any arm swing you'll see a perfect arc. Remove arcs — instant robot look. That's why badly-made games feel "stiff."
Timing: Wile E. Coyote runs off a cliff, hangs in mid-air (pause = timing comedy), THEN looks down, THEN falls. The pause is 12 frames of "timing" — that pause IS the joke.
Exaggeration: Tom & Jerry — when Tom is hit, his eyes pop out of his skull. His teeth fly out. That's exaggerated PAIN — more fun to watch than realistic pain.
Appeal: Darth Vader is a villain but has MASSIVE appeal — iconic silhouette, imposing movement, distinctive sound. Thanos in Avengers — Marvel spent years making him feel weighty and purposeful.
🎯 POINT AT: The 12-number counter visible on the slide — "all 12 principles covered. These were written in 1981 and are STILL the global standard."

08 · ANIMATION SPACE
↗ Open Slide 12
  • ▶SVG diagrams at top: "Left — 2D has only X and Y. Middle — 2.5D looks 3D but is really 2D layers. Right — 3D has X, Y AND Z — true depth."
  • ▶2D: "Flat, like drawing on paper. Tom & Jerry, original Super Mario, Clash of Clans — all 2D. Simple to create, low hardware requirement."
  • ▶2.5D: "Technically still 2D sprites, but parallax scrolling makes it feel 3D. When you scroll in Paper Mario — foreground moves faster than background, tricking your brain into seeing depth."
  • ▶3D: "True depth, real volume, any camera angle. Toy Story, Frozen, Fortnite, COD. The camera can orbit 360° around any object — impossible in 2D."
🎮 GAMES YOU KNOW — WHICH SPACE?
2D: Angry Birds, Clash Royale, Subway Surfers (2D sprites on 3D rail), old Pokémon games, Street Fighter
2.5D: Paper Mario, Ori and the Blind Forest, Little Big Planet, Hollow Knight, Cuphead
3D: PUBG, Fortnite, Call of Duty, FIFA, every Pixar/DreamWorks film, GTA
Fun fact: Clash of Clans uses 2.5D — the buildings look 3D but are actually 2D sprites drawn from an angle. That's why you can't rotate the map.

GOLDEN RULES — All Three Presenters

  • Don't read the slide — the slide is your visual aid. You talk to the AUDIENCE, not the screen. If you're reading, you've lost them.
  • Point at specific parts while talking — use your finger or a pointer. It directs the audience's eye exactly where you want it.
  • 2–3 minutes per slide — don't rush (you'll look nervous), don't drag (they'll get bored).
  • Use the "Open Slide" buttons above to jump directly to any slide during your presentation
  • Phone navigation: Swipe left/right on the presentation to go between slides
  • Use real examples from daily life — WhatsApp animations, PUBG, Instagram, Clash of Clans. Audience connects to what they know.
  • Physical demos work: Squash your fist (principle 1), swing your arm in an arc (principle 7), reach for something (show IK). These visual demos are remembered for days.
  • Handoff lines matter: "Now Taha will explain..." and "Abdul Rahman will take us deeper..." — smooth handoffs make you look professional.
  • Unknown question? Say: "Good question — let me check / that's outside my section, [name] can answer that". Never guess.

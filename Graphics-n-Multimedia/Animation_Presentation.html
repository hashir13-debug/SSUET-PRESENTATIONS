01
INTRODUCTION TO ANIMATION
Definition · Brain Effects · FPS Standards
Hashir
Slide 3
DEFINITION
  • Still images shown rapidly = illusion of motion
  • Brain is "tricked" by two biological effects working together
  • First animation: Muybridge horse gallop (1878)
👁 PERSISTENCE OF VISION

Physical eye effect — image stays ~50ms after disappearing. Afterimages overlap → smooth flow.

Peter Roget, 1824

🧠 PHI PHENOMENON

Psychological effect — brain INFERS motion between two still objects. Works even without eye retention.

Wertheimer, 1912

ANIMATION TECHNIQUES
3 Core Methods
Hashir
Slide 5
1910s–1990s
CEL ANIMATION
• Draw on transparent plastic sheets
• Each sheet = 1 frame
• Layer over background
• Hand-filmed, frame by frame
Tom & Jerry · Snow White · Bambi
1970s–Now
PATH ANIMATION
• Draw path first
• Object follows automatically
• Straight or curved paths
• Add easing (slow in/out)
CSS · After Effects · Unity
1980s–Now
COMPUTER ANIMATION
• All digital creation
• Simulates cel & path
• Physics simulation built-in
• Real-time or pre-rendered
Pixar · DreamWorks · Fortnite

4A
TYPES: CEL ANIMATION
Keyframes · Tweening · Pencil Test
Taha
Slide 6
KEYFRAMES
  • Most extreme/important poses
  • Drawn by SENIOR animators
  • Like checkpoints in the action
TWEENING
  • Frames BETWEEN keyframes
  • Done by JUNIOR animators
  • Software auto-tweens now!
PENCIL TEST
  • Rough sketches filmed & played back FIRST
  • Check timing before expensive inking & painting
  • Every major studio still does this

4B
TYPES: PATH ANIMATION
Object follows a predefined trajectory automatically
Taha
Slide 7
6 STEPS TO CREATE PATH ANIMATION:
1. Draw the PATH — straight or curved
2. Set START and END points
3. Set DURATION (how long)
4. Apply EASING — slow in / slow out
5. Set orientation — does it face direction?
6. Preview, adjust & render
Used in: CSS animations · After Effects · Unity · SVG paths · Game projectiles

05
COMPUTER ANIMATION
Cel vs Computer · FK / IK · Morphing
Taha
Slide 8
CEL ANIMATION COMPUTER ANIMATION
Hand-drawn on plastic sheetsCreated in digital software
Every frame drawn manually — slowAuto-tweening & physics built-in
Physical film — expensive & fragileDigital files — share instantly
Months per 1 minute of filmReal-time preview — much faster
FK (Forward)
Move parent → children follow. Like a puppet.
Best for: robots, flags, tails
IK (Inverse)
Move end point → joints auto-calculate. Like reaching for a cup.
Best for: walking, grabbing, games
Morphing
Map points on source & target → smooth shape transformation.
MJ Black or White · T-1000

7A
12 PRINCIPLES — PART 1 (1–6)
Disney (1981) · Still the Universal Gold Standard
Abdul
Slide 10
01
SQUASH & STRETCH
Squash on IMPACT, stretch when moving FAST. Volume always stays constant!
Rubber ball bounce. Most fundamental.
02
ANTICIPATION
Small OPPOSITE action before the main action. Crouches before jump. Winds up before throw.
Without it, actions feel sudden.
03
STAGING
One clear idea at a time. Clear silhouette, camera serves story, direct viewer's eye.
Theater staging, not crowded photo.
04
STRAIGHT AHEAD vs POSE-TO-POSE
SA = draw every frame → fluid (fire/water). PtP = key poses first → controlled (character acting).
Best work combines both.
05
FOLLOW THROUGH & OVERLAP
Parts keep moving AFTER main stops. Hair swings after head stops. Different parts, different rates.
Makes motion feel organic & alive.
06
EASE IN & EASE OUT
Slow start + slow end. Nothing in nature starts at full speed. Linear = robotic. Ease = natural.
Most impactful for 'weight' feel.
[ IMAGE: Rubber ball showing squash on landing & stretch in air ]
Classic squash and stretch example
7B
12 PRINCIPLES — PART 2 (7–12)
"Exaggeration of truth" — Walt Disney
Abdul
Slide 11
07
ARCS
All natural motion follows ARC paths. Arms, heads, eyes all move in arcs. Parabola for thrown objects.
Exception: robots move in straight lines.
08
SECONDARY ACTION
Supporting actions enrich main movement. Walking + arms swing. Running + hair flows. Must NOT overpower main.
Supports — never steals focus.
09
TIMING
More frames = slower, heavier. Fewer = faster, lighter. Blink: 6f. Head turn: 12f. Heavy punch: 3f.
Wrong timing = nothing else matters.
10
EXAGGERATION
Push beyond realistic limits. NOT unrealistic — making TRUTH more visible. Sad = cries buckets, not small tears.
Exaggerate the ESSENCE.
11
SOLID DRAWING
Characters must feel 3D even in 2D. Understand cubes, spheres, cylinders. Avoid "twinning" (mirror poses).
Strong line of action in every pose.
12
APPEAL
Character must be interesting to WATCH. Not just cuteness — villains have appeal too! Clear design, strong silhouette.
Would audience want to keep watching?

08
ANIMATION SPACE
2D · 2½D · 3D — Three Types of Spatial Dimensions
Abdul
Slide 12
2D SPACE
X + Y axes only
• Flat — like paper
• Simple to create
• Low hardware needs
• Classic cartoon look

Tom & Jerry · Mario · Mobile games

Adobe Animate, Toon Boom
2½D SPACE
2D + depth illusion
• Parallax scrolling
• Layers fake depth
• Still technically 2D
• "Pseudo-3D"

Paper Mario · Ori · South Park

After Effects Z-depth
3D SPACE
X + Y + Z axes
• True depth & volume
• Any camera angle
• Realistic lighting
• Needs powerful GPU

Toy Story · Frozen · Fortnite

Blender, Maya, Unreal
